## What is anxiety?

**Anxiety** is a natural response of the body to situations perceived as threatening. In moderate doses, it is adaptive—it keeps us alert and motivated. The problem arises when it becomes chronic, disproportionate, or interferes with daily life.

According to the World Health Organization, anxiety disorders affect more than **3.6% of the global population** and are among the most prevalent mental health conditions worldwide.

## Physical symptoms of anxiety

The body is often the first place anxiety leaves its mark:

- **Muscle tension**: especially in the neck, shoulders and jaw
- **Heart palpitations** or racing heartbeat
- **Difficulty breathing** or feeling of tightness in the chest
- **Excessive sweating** without physical cause
- **Dizziness or feeling unsteady**
- **Digestive issues**: nausea, diarrhea, irritable bowel syndrome
- **Insomnia** or restless, non-restorative sleep
- **Chronic fatigue** despite getting adequate rest

Many people visit doctors for these physical symptoms without realizing they have an anxiety component. It is important to rule out physical causes, but also to consider emotional health.

## Emotional symptoms

On an emotional level, anxiety can manifest as:

- **Persistent restlessness or nervousness** without clear cause
- **Disproportionate irritability** in response to minor setbacks
- **Sense of impending danger** or constant foreboding
- **Inability to relax**, even in calm situations
- **Fear of losing control**

## Cognitive symptoms

The anxious mind has characteristic thinking patterns:

- **Catastrophizing**: anticipating the worst possible outcome
- **Overgeneralization**: "this always happens to me"
- **Rumination**: repetitive thoughts that are hard to interrupt
- **Difficulty concentrating**: the mind cannot focus
- **Hypervigilance**: constantly scanning the environment for threats

## When is it necessary to seek help?

The line between normal anxiety and an anxiety disorder is not always clear. These are indicators that speaking with a professional may be appropriate:

1. Symptoms last **more than 6 months**
2. They significantly interfere with work, relationships, or daily life
3. They trigger **avoidance behaviors** (stopping activities out of fear)
4. They do not respond to basic management strategies (exercise, breathing, sleep)

## Management strategies

Before seeking professional help, or as a complement to it, these strategies have demonstrated effectiveness:

- **Regular exercise**: reduces cortisol levels and releases endorphins
- **Breathing techniques**: diaphragmatic breathing activates the parasympathetic nervous system
- **Mindfulness and meditation**: train the ability to observe thoughts without fusing with them
- **Sleep hygiene**: maintaining regular schedules and limiting screens before bed
- **Reducing caffeine and alcohol**: both substances amplify anxiety

## Conclusion

Detecting anxiety early is the first step to managing it effectively. If you recognize several of these symptoms in yourself, do not ignore them. Anxiety is treatable, and with the right support, it is possible to recover your well-being.

Remember: seeking help is an act of courage, not weakness.
