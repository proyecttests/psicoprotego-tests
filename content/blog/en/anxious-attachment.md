## What is anxious attachment?

**Anxious attachment** is a pattern of emotional bonding that develops in childhood and persists into adulthood. People with this style tend to experience intense fear of abandonment, seek constant reassurance from their partners, and feel overwhelming anxiety about the stability of their relationships.

Attachment theory, developed by John Bowlby in the 1960s, established that early experiences with our caregivers shape how we relate to others throughout life. When those experiences are inconsistent—sometimes available, sometimes absent—the attachment system learns to remain on permanent alert.

## Symptoms of anxious attachment in adults

Recognizing anxious attachment can be the first step toward healthier relationships. These are the most common indicators:

- **Intense fear of abandonment**: you ruminate about your partner leaving, even without any real trigger.
- **Constant need for reassurance**: you seek confirmation of being loved, but the relief never lasts long.
- **Emotional hypervigilance**: you interpret gestures, tone of voice, or silence as signals of rejection.
- **Jealousy and possessiveness**: insecurity can translate into controlling behaviors.
- **Difficulty being alone**: solitude feels threatening rather than restorative.
- **High emotional reactivity**: conflicts are experienced with disproportionate intensity.

## Causes: where does anxious attachment come from?

Anxious attachment usually has roots in childhood, particularly in environments where care was **unpredictable or inconsistent**. The child learned that affection could appear or disappear without clear reason, generating a constant state of alert.

Other contributing factors:

- Early separations or losses
- Caregivers with their own anxiety patterns
- Emotional abandonment (even when caregivers were physically present)
- Previous adult relationships with emotionally unavailable partners

## Can you change your attachment style?

The good news is yes. Although attachment styles are deeply ingrained patterns, they are not fixed. Psychotherapy—especially schema therapy or attachment-focused therapy—can help you develop a **more secure attachment style**.

Helpful strategies also include:

1. Practicing **mindfulness** to observe reactions without acting impulsively.
2. Learning to **regulate emotions** before seeking reassurance from your partner.
3. Communicating needs directly and assertively.
4. Building an **internal sense of security** that does not depend solely on another person.

## Conclusion

Understanding anxious attachment is not about labeling yourself—it is about recognizing a pattern that may have been influencing your relationships for years. With the right information and appropriate support, it is possible to build more secure and fulfilling connections.
